The daylighting exit arrays (`UnfilteredExitProbabilityDaylight`, `InitiationProbabilityDaylight`, `ProgressionProbabilityDaylight`, `SrpDaylight`) are null when no daylighting exit path is evaluated (conditions 1 through 4). They are populated only for conditions 5 and 6.

## Interpreting Results

### SRP as a Fragility Curve

The `SrpGoverning` array represents a fragility curve &mdash; the conditional probability of breach due to backward erosion piping as a function of headwater elevation. The SRP is expected to be monotonically non-decreasing with headwater elevation because higher pool levels produce higher driving heads and greater seepage pressures.

### Which Path Governs

When both paths are evaluated, the governing SRP at each headwater level is the maximum of the toe and daylight SRPs. In practice:

- The **toe path** often governs when a confining layer is present because the `ExitIsHole = true` setting reduces the Schmertmann critical gradient by a factor of 2 (via $C_{3d} = 0.5$), resulting in a higher probability of progression.
- The **daylight path** may govern at lower headwater levels if the daylighting exit path has significantly higher P(unfiltered exit) or P(initiation) than the toe path. However, in conditions 5 and 6, the daylight path sets both to 1.0 while the toe path uses BTCase4-computed values, so the daylight path's progression probability (which is lower due to the longer seepage path) typically prevents it from governing.

### Sensitivity to Key Inputs

The following inputs have the greatest influence on the screening SRP:

- **Flaw likelihood** &mdash; Directly scales the entire SRP. Moving from Neutral (0.50) to VeryLikely (0.99) approximately doubles the SRP.
- **Foundation thickness (DDistribution)** &mdash; Affects both BTCase4 (seepage path denominator) and Schmertmann (depth correction C_D). Thicker foundations increase the seepage path length and generally reduce gradients.
- **Blanket thickness (BlanketThicknessDistribution)** &mdash; Directly controls the vertical exit gradient in BTCase4. Thinner blankets produce higher gradients and higher P(initiation) or P(unfiltered exit).
- **Relative density category** &mdash; Controls the density correction C_gamma in Schmertmann. Denser soils have higher piping resistance.
- **USCS classification** &mdash; Controls roof support probability. Clean sands and gravels (GP, SP) have P(roof) = 0.01, effectively preventing progression. Clays (CL, CH) have P(roof) = 1.0.

### Screening vs. Detailed Analysis

The screening SRP should be interpreted as a conservative estimate intended to identify structures that warrant further analysis. A high screening SRP does not necessarily indicate a high risk of failure &mdash; it indicates that the structure should be evaluated with the full BEP toolbox models, where:

- The actual blanket geometry can be specified (eliminating the infinite blanket assumption).
- The appropriate BT Case (1 through 8) can be selected for the site configuration.
- Anisotropy, exit angle, and underlayer corrections can be applied to the Schmertmann model.
- The Sellmeijer progression method is available as an alternative.
- Monte Carlo settings can be adjusted for precision requirements.

A low screening SRP provides reasonable confidence that backward erosion piping is not a critical concern for the structure at the screening level.
