# Monte Carlo Simulation

The model uses Monte Carlo simulation to propagate material property uncertainty through the erosion model and compute the probability of breach at
each overtopping depth. This chapter describes the correlated sampling method, the simulation execution framework, and the assembly of system response
curves from simulation results.

## Correlated Material Property Sampling

The three material properties — erodibility coefficient ($K_d$), critical shear stress ($\tau_c$), and Manning's roughness ($n$) — are not sampled
independently. Physical relationships between these properties require correlated sampling to produce realistic combinations of material parameters.

### Gaussian Copula

The model uses a Gaussian copula to generate correlated samples while preserving each variable's marginal distribution. The copula operates in
standard normal space and maps the correlated normal samples back to the original distribution space through inverse CDF transformation.

### Correlation Matrix Structure

The 3×3 correlation matrix has the following structure:

<Equation equationKey="overtopping-screening-eq-correlation-matrix" equation="\mathbf{R} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & \rho_{K_d,\tau_c} & \rho_{K_d,\tau_c} \cdot \rho_{\tau_c,n} \\ \rho_{K_d,\tau_c} & 1 & \rho_{\tau_c,n} \\ \rho_{K_d,\tau_c} \cdot \rho_{\tau_c,n} & \rho_{\tau_c,n} & 1 \end{bmatrix}" />

With the default correlations ($\rho_{K_d,\tau_c} = -0.6$, $\rho_{\tau_c,n} = -0.2$), the matrix evaluates to:

<EquationNoRef equation="\mathbf{R} = \begin{bmatrix} 1.0 & -0.6 & 0.12 \\ -0.6 & 1.0 & -0.2 \\ 0.12 & -0.2 & 1.0 \end{bmatrix}" />

The matrix is enforced to be symmetric positive-definite using `MatrixRegularization.MakeSymmetricPositiveDefinite()` from the RMC.Numerics library.
This is implemented in `OvertoppingModel.BuildCorrelationMatrix()`.

### Latin Hypercube Sampling

The model uses Latin Hypercube sampling (LHS) rather than simple random sampling to generate the multivariate normal variates. LHS provides better
coverage of the sample space with fewer iterations by stratifying each marginal distribution into equal-probability intervals and sampling once from
each interval. This reduces the number of iterations needed for stable probability estimates compared to simple random sampling.

## Simulation Execution

The Monte Carlo simulation evaluates every combination of realization (material property set) and hazard level (overtopping depth). The simulation is
structured as an outer loop over realizations and an inner loop over hazard levels.

### Parallelization

The outer loop over realizations is parallelized using `Parallel.For`, which distributes realizations across available processor cores. Each
realization is independent — the material properties are drawn once per realization and reused across all hazard levels — so no synchronization is
required between threads.

## Response Curve Assembly

After all realizations complete, the model aggregates the boolean results (grass removed, breach occurred) across realizations at each hazard level.

### Breach Probability

The breach probability at each hazard level is computed as the proportion of realizations that resulted in breach:

<Equation equationKey="overtopping-screening-eq-breach-prob" equation="P(\text{breach} \mid H) = \frac{\text{number of breach events at depth } H}{R}" />

where $R$ is the total number of Monte Carlo iterations. This produces the primary system response curve stored in `ResponseFunction`.

The grass removal and conditional breach response curves allow engineers to decompose the overall breach probability into its two constituent phases:
the probability that overtopping flow is sufficient to remove the grass cover, and the probability that the exposed soil erodes to breach given grass
removal. This decomposition can help identify whether the grass cover or the soil erodibility is the dominant factor controlling the system response
at a given overtopping depth.
