# Assumptions and Limitations

The overtopping erosion screening model applies several simplifying assumptions to enable rapid, low-input screening analysis. These assumptions are
deliberate design decisions for screening-level use — they reduce input requirements and computational complexity while capturing the dominant physics
of overtopping erosion. Each assumption is documented with its rationale, effect on results, and the conditions under which it may not hold.

## Key Limitation: Duration Scaling

The duration scaling relationship (assumption 3 in <TableReference tableKey="overtopping-screening-table-assumptions" />) is one of the most impactful
screening assumptions. The model requires only a single overtopping duration at the reference depth and extrapolates all other durations using a
fixed power-law exponent. This approach assumes that the relationship between overtopping depth and duration follows a consistent pattern across all
reservoirs, which is not the case in practice.

Reservoirs with large storage volumes relative to inflow tend to have longer-duration overtopping events that increase slowly with depth. Reservoirs
with small storage volumes tend to have shorter, more sharply peaked events. The power-law exponent of $1/1.8$ was selected as a reasonable
middle-ground approximation, but engineers should evaluate whether the resulting durations are plausible for their specific reservoir. If the scaled
durations appear unreasonable, the base duration should be adjusted until the scaling produces realistic values across the range of overtopping
depths.

## Key Limitation: Uniform Erosion

The uniform erosion assumption (assumption 2 in <TableReference tableKey="overtopping-screening-table-assumptions" />) is a fundamental simplification
of the actual erosion process. In reality, overtopping erosion of embankment dams often proceeds through headcut formation and migration — a
concentrated erosion process where a steep scarp forms at the downstream toe and migrates upstream through the embankment. The headcut erosion
mechanism is modeled by detailed software such as WinDAM C<Citation citationKey="WinDAM" /> but is not captured by this screening model.

The uniform erosion assumption means that the model distributes the erosive energy across the entire downstream face rather than concentrating it at a
headcut. This can produce different breach timing compared to headcut models. However, for screening purposes, the model has been validated against
Quantitative Risk Assessment (QRA) response functions from multiple USACE dam safety studies to ensure that screening results are reasonable
relative to detailed assessments.

## Scope Exclusions

The following capabilities are explicitly outside the scope of the screening model:

- **Rockfill embankments** — The erodibility classifications and erosion equations are calibrated for earth embankments. Rockfill materials have
  fundamentally different erosion behavior.
- **Riprap removal** — Modeling the stability and progressive removal of riprap armoring is planned as a future enhancement but is not currently
  supported.
- **Headcut erosion** — The model uses a surface erosion approach and does not model the discrete headcut erosion mechanism.
- **Zoned embankments** — The model treats the embankment as a single homogeneous material. Internal zoning, filters, and drains are not represented.
- **Non-erodible elements** — Cutoff walls, sheet pile, concrete caps, and other structural features that would arrest erosion cannot be modeled.

## Monte Carlo Reproducibility

The screening model uses a configurable random number generator seed (`RngSeed`, default 42) to ensure that identical inputs produce identical outputs.
This design choice supports regression testing and reproducible screening results. Setting the seed to null enables non-deterministic sampling, which
produces slightly different results on each run due to different random number sequences.
