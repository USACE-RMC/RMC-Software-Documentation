Levee Screening Tool

Hazards Module

Purpose of this module​

A critical component of risk analyses is the degree of hazard imposed upon the system of interest, which in this case includes the levee segment being screened. Hazard is distinct from the response of system components (which are affected by hazard) and from the consequences (which result from distress or failure of system components); risk is a measure of how hazard affects system response and possible resultant consequences. For screening-level risk assessments using the LST, flood (hydrologic) hazard is the only hazard included. Seismic hazard should be discussed outside of the LST application for those rare occasions when they may drive system risk.

Information about the flood hazard is entered in the Hazards Module. This module has two tabs: the General Information tab and the Loading Functions tab. The Loading Functions tab is where you enter the quantitative data used in the LST risk compute about the flood hazard, which is how often the levee experiences different levels of loading. For the purposes of the LST, the hazard is described as the annual exceedance probability (AEP) of peak stages for the entire range of flows that could load the levee. The LST does not consider seismic hazards.

Enter data about the age and methods used to estimate the Hazard Curves in the General Information tab. This provides the reviewers and decision-makers information necessary to gage the quality and level of uncertainty associated with the hazard component of risk.

Data and information guidelines, requirements, and sources​

Follow these guidelines about collecting and processing data and information:

  1. Base hydrologic and hydraulic inputs to the LST on readily available existing information
  2. Do not undertake new or updated analyses requiring more than a few hours’ worth of effort to obtain estimates of required inputs
  3. When no data or information exists, use professional judgment to estimate the required inputs and provide details in the appropriate Comments field
  4. Obtain data and information from published sources whenever possible
  5. Have a qualified hydraulic engineer on the screening team with experience in levee analysis and design review the data and information before using them in the levee screening process
  6. Consider any data or information that supports an improved estimate for the hydrologic and hydraulic inputs
  7. Although multiple data and information sources can be used and compared to improve the quality of the estimate, select a primary source to describe as input to the LST
  8. Document data and information sources in the applicable Comments field

The types of hydrologic and hydraulic data and information that may be required for the levee assessment include:

Top-of-levee water surface elevation and/or discharge
Authorized-capacity water surface elevation and/or discharge
Levee-toe water surface elevation and/or discharge
Closure sill elevations and/or discharge
Floodwall toe water surface elevations and/or discharge
Discharge-probability relationships
Water surface elevation-discharge (rating) relationships
Water surface elevation-probability relationships

Sources of data and information for the hydrologic and hydraulic inputs include, but are not limited to:

USACE Digital Project Notebook
Project drawings and reports
Design memoranda
Local or regional flood frequency studies and reports
Historical flood records
US Geological Survey surface water data
National Weather Service Advanced Hydrologic Prediction Service
Federal Emergency Management Agency Map Service Center
USACE National Levee Database

General Information tab​

Tab overview​

The General Information tab consists of three sections:

  • Design Information
  • Historic Loading Information
  • Data Sources and Methods
Information related to the authorized capacity of the levee and considerations for future settlement or subsidence.
Often, the levee has experienced historic loads above given levels. This section provides to opportunity to add historic loading information.
Describe the data sources and methods that are used to estimate the AEP values entered on the Hazard Curves tab.

Most of the answers are chosen from a given list of values, and each question is accompanied by a Comments text field for explaining the answer selected.

How the information on the General Information tab is used​

The information entered on the General Information tab is used to assess the quality and quantity of available information for both individual levee segments and from a portfolio perspective. The information may be used to inform levee risk classification by identifying and characterizing potential sources of uncertainty. It can also be used to identify data gaps for the purpose of defining the scope and priority of further studies. None of the information entered on this tab is used in the LST quantitative risk compute, but it is important that the screening team complete all fields as accurately as possible.

Design Information​

Enter the following information about the original design of the levee:

  1. 1The Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP) for water surface elevation at the current authorized capacity of the levee. The AEP for the water surface elevation at the authorized capacity is the AEP at which the levee system would begin to exceed its current authorized (or design) capacity. To determine and enter the representative ACE value:
    1. aDetermine the levee system’s authorized capacity based on the hydrologic information, planning policy, design criteria, and project authorizations that were in effect during the project formulation and design. For projects formulated under the current National Economic Development (NED) policy in ER 1105-2-101, the authorized capacity would typically be the top of levee. Note that levees authorized under the current NED framework do not provide any allowance for freeboard.
    2. bEstimate the water surface elevation at the current authorized (or design) capacity. Estimate the AEP for current hydrologic and hydraulic conditions using available historical flood records, discharge-probability, water surface elevation-probability, elevation-discharge, and water surface profile-probability relationships. Note the hydrologic and hydraulic conditions, data sources, station location, etc., in the Comments field, ensuring all elevations are in NAVD88. If the project contains both embankment and floodwall alignments be sure to note both best estimates in the Comments field. In particular:
      1. iBase the water surface elevation estimate on the original authorization or design of the project and any authorization or design modifications that have been implemented since original construction, such as an authorized levee raise.
      2. iiEstimate the water surface elevation for current hydrologic and hydraulic conditions.
      3. iiiIf the levee authorization is based on a specific historical profile or water surface elevation, base the authorized capacity on that profile or water surface elevation.
      4. ivIf the levee authorization is based on a specific probability or historical discharge, base the estimate of the authorized capacity elevation on the water surface elevation under current hydrologic conditions for that probability or discharge.
      5. vUse drawings, field surveys, historical floods, stage and/or discharge data, water surface profiles, or other available appropriate sources to estimate authorized capacity water surface elevation.
      6. viWhen no information is available, estimate the water surface elevation at 3 feet below the top of the levee.
  2. 2Determine whether future settlement or subsidence is likely to significantly change the current overtopping ACE if nothing is done to address it. In the Comments field, provide justification for your answer. If answer is “Yes”, describe plans to address it if any exist.

Historic Loading Information​

Enter the following information about historic loadings on the levee segment. These values should be put in context of the current levee alignment and height. For each entry, use the Comments section to describe how the entered value was estimated.

  1. 3How many times a flood loading has exceeded 25% of the total levee height.
  2. 4How many times a flood loading has exceeded 50% of the total levee height.
  3. 5How many times a flood loading has exceeded 75% of the total levee height.
  4. 6How many times a flood loading has exceeded 100% of the total levee height (i.e. loading exceeding existing crest profile not considering any emergency levee raises).
  5. 7How many times has the levee actually overtopped?
  6. 8Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP) for the largest historical flood that has occurred since construction of the levee. This input is the AEP for the water surface elevation of the largest historical flood (defined by water surface elevation) that has occurred since the construction of the levee system to its current configuration. To estimate the AEP value:
    1. aEstimate this water surface elevation based on available high water marks, gage records, profiles, or other data. When detailed water surface elevation information is not available, the estimate can be made based on published estimates of annual chance exceedance for the specific event.
    2. bBase the AEP estimate on current hydrologic and hydraulic conditions using available historical flood records, discharge-probability, water surface elevation-probability, elevation-discharge (rating), and water surface profile-probability relationships.
    3. cNote when the event occurred and observed discharges and/or water surface elevations in the Comments field. If the project contains both embankment and floodwall alignments be sure to note both best estimates in the Comments field.
  7. 9The representative loading associated with the largest historical flood as a percentage of the levee height. Importantly, this should be defined as the estimate of the water surface elevation of the largest historical flood with the levee in its current configuration. To estimate and enter this input into the LST:
    1. aEstimate and enter the loading based on current hydrologic and hydraulic conditions using available historic, discharge-probability, water surface elevation-probability, water surface elevation-discharge (rating), and water surface profile probability relationships.
    2. bEnter information about the year, hydrologic and hydraulic conditions, data sources, and the like in the Comments field. If the project contains both embankment and floodwall alignments be sure to note both best estimates in the Comments field. If larger flood happened prior to major modifications to the levee, describe those in the comments as well.

Flood Characteristics​

  1. 10Can the levee be flanked?
  2. 11A description of the flood duration characteristics for a large (near 100%) hydraulic flood load on this levee. To to answer these questions, consider the characteristics of flood events that generate a peak water surface elevation approaching the top of the levee. The value entered here should reflect the amount of time that the loading is above 50-75% of the total levee height, and should be in agreement with the durations provided in the consequences tab for the 100% load scenario. To determine and enter this description in the LST:
    1. aDetermine the typical flood loading from current hydrologic and hydraulic conditions using available historic, discharge-probability, water surface elevation-probability, water surface elevation-discharge, and water surface profile probability relationships.

    1. aIf the primary loading source is coastal, please contact the LST Helpdesk (LSTHelpDesk@usace.army.mil) for updated guidance on developing loading functions for coastal levees.

Data Sources and Methods​

Enter supporting information about the source and age of the data used to develop the loading functions.

  1. 14Estimate the age of the primary hydrologic (discharge) data used in developing the loading functions and select the appropriate age range. The possible input values are:
    &lt; 10 years
    10-19 years
    20-29 years
    ≥ 30 years

    If the data are unpublished or a date is not readily available, use the best available information to estimate the age of the data.

  2. 15Select the analytical method from which the discharge value was derived from the following choices:
    1. No data exist or are readily available for the levee system and the AEP estimate is based on engineering judgment by the hydraulic engineer on the assessment team.
    2. The levee system is on an ungaged river system and a regional regression equation has been used to estimate the AEP at an ungaged site. Typical references include Chapter 9 of EM 1110-2-1415 and the USGS National Flood Frequency Program.
    3. The levee system is on an ungaged river system and discharge-probability data from a nearby gage located within the watershed has been used. Here, the AEP would be estimated by transferring data from a gaged site to an ungaged site and applying a drainage area ratio. Acceptable sources of gage data and gaged-ungaged relationships include the USGS and local districts.
    4. The levee system is on a gaged river reach and a graphical discharge-probability analysis for a gaged site has been used. One acceptable method is to use relationships developed in accordance with Chapter 2 of EM 1110-2-1415. If gage data are readily available, a cursory graphical probability function can be developed using HEC-SSP.
    5. The levee system is on a gaged river reach and an analytical frequency analysis of discharge for a gaged site has been used. One acceptable method is to use relationships developed in accordance with Chapter 3 of EM 1110-2-1415 or Bulletin 17B. If gage data are readily available, a cursory analytical probability function can be developed using HEC-SSP.
    6. The levee system is on a river system that is either gaged or ungaged, and an approximate rainfall runoff model has been used. That is, the model applies estimated or regional runoff parameters without calibration of the model. The AEP estimate is based on the rainfall runoff results. Acceptable methods include models developed in accordance with EM 1110-2-1417. If an existing model is not readily available, this method will generally not be used for screening.
    7. The levee system is on a river system that is either gaged or ungaged, and a calibrated rainfall runoff model has been used. The calibrated model applies site-specific runoff parameters with calibration of the parameters against historical or other appropriate storm data. The AEP estimate is based on the rainfall-runoff model results. Acceptable methods include models developed in accordance with EM 1110-2-1417. If an existing model is not readily available, this method will generally not be used for screening.
    8. No discharge data has been used to develop the AEP estimate. For example, this choice is selected when a levee system is authorized for a specific water surface elevation profile or when a water surface elevation-probability analysis is used instead of a discharge-probability and elevation-discharge function. Provide information about the discharge data’s age and analytical method in the Comments field.

    Provide information about the discharge data's age and analytical method in the Comments field.

  3. 16Determine the age of the primary hydraulic (elevation) data used in estimating the LST inputs. Select the age range based on the publication date of the data’s source. If the data are unpublished or a date is not readily available, use the best available information to estimate the age of the data. The possible input values are:
    &lt; 10 years
    10-19 years
    20-29 years
    ≥ 30 years

    If the data are unpublished or a date is not readily available, use the best available information to estimate the age of the data.

  4. 17Select the analytical method from which the water surface elevation was derived from the following choices:
    1. No data exist or are readily available for the levee system and the AEP estimate is based on engineering judgment by the hydraulic engineer on the assessment team.
    2. The levee system is gaged and a graphical water surface elevation-probability analysis has been used to determine the water surface elevation. One acceptable method is to use relationships developed in accordance with Chapter 2 of EM 1110-2-1415. If gage data are readily available, a cursory graphical probability function can be developed using HEC-SSP. The gage site should be identified in the Comments field.
    3. A levee system is gaged and the AEP has been estimated based on a discharge probability and a water surface elevation-discharge relationship. Acceptable methods include relationships developed from discharge measurements, gage ratings, and historical flood data. If gage data are readily available, a cursory water surface elevation-discharge relationship can be developed.
    4. A levee system is either gaged or ungaged and a normal depth estimate has been used. The AEP estimate should be based on surveyed cross section data and a normal depth estimate using Manning’s equation. If bathymetric survey data are readily available, a cursory normal depth estimate can be made.
    5. The levee system is either gaged or ungaged and a limited steady flow hydraulic model has been used. The limited steady flow hydraulic model could be developed from light detection and ranging (LIDAR) surveys or similar terrain data with limited field inspections and data collection. Acceptable methods include models developed in accordance with Paragraph C.4.3 of Guidelines and specifications for flood hazard mapping partners (FEMA, last updated 2011). If an existing model is not readily available, this method will generally not be used for screening. Reference to the model used and date of modeling should be made in the Comments field.
    6. The levee system is either gaged or ungaged and a detailed steady flow hydraulic model has been used. The detailed steady flow hydraulic model could be developed from light detection and ranging (LIDAR) surveys or similar terrain data, combined with field inspections, surveys, and data collection to define the geometry of the model and all pertinent hydraulic structures. Acceptable methods include models developed in accordance with Paragraph C.3 of Guidelines and specifications for flood hazard mapping partners. If an existing model is not readily available, this method will generally not be used for screening. Reference to the model used and date of modeling should be made in the Comments field.
    7. The levee system is either gaged or ungaged and an unsteady flow hydraulic model has been used. The unsteady flow hydraulic model could be a 1- or 2-dimensional model. Acceptable methods include models developed in accordance with paragraphs C.3.4.1 and C.3.4.2 of Guidelines and specifications for flood hazard mapping partners. If an existing model is not readily available, this method will generally not be used for screening. Reference to the model used and date of modeling should be made in the Comments field.
    8. When none of the other choices apply, this option is selected. The specific method should be described in the Comments field. Provide information about the discharge data’s age and analytical method in the Comments field.

    Provide information about the discharge data's age and analytical method in the Comments field.

  6. 19Describe the scope of the FIS with one of these three choices:
    Approximate
    Detailed
    N/A

    The scope of the FIS should be documented in the FIS report itself. It must be consistent with the requirements for limited and detailed studies in Guidelines and specifications for flood hazard mapping partners.

  8. 21Determine the age of the primary interior drainage data used in estimating the LST inputs. Select the age range based on the publication date of the interior drainage data source. If the data are unpublished or a date is not readily available, use the best available information to estimate the age of the data. If interior drainage information does not exist, select n/a.The possible input values are:
    &lt; 10 years
    10-19 years
    20-29 years
    ≥ 30 years
    n/a

    If the data are unpublished or a date is not readily available, use the best available information to estimate the age of the data. If interior drainage information does not exist, select n/a.

  9. 22Select the analytical method of the most recent interior drainage analysis from the following choices:
    1. Here, a design rainfall intensity and discharge is based on the rational or other appropriate method. Interior drainage features are then sized and designed to accommodate this design discharge.
    2. Here, a rainfall-runoff model is used to obtain design hydrographs for a specific frequency rainfall event. Interior drainage features are then sized and designed to accommodate the event by routing the hydrographs through the interior system.
    3. This method includes the analysis of the full period of record, multiple discrete historical events, and stochastically generated continuous records. Acceptable methods include analyses done in accordance with EM 1110-2-1413.
    4. This method considers the coincident probability of interior and exterior stages. Acceptable methods include analyses done in accordance with EM 1110-2-1413.
    5. Select this choice when no interior drainage analysis has been done.
    6. If none of the other methods apply, select this choice and describe the specific method in the Comments field.

Loading Functions tab​

Tab overview​

A loading function is also commonly referred to as a hazard curve or stage-frequency curve. Data entered on this tab are used in the risk compute. At least one loading function is required. The loading function should represent the AEP for different water levels at the location on the levee segment where performance issues are most likely to occur.

For most levee screenings, a single loading function that represents the AEP of different loading heights along the levee will be adequate. However, if the AEP vs. loading height changes significantly along the segment it may be necessary to enter more than one loading function if one of the following situations exist:

  1. 1The loading function where concerns about one performance mode (e.g. closures or floodwalls) exists is significantly different that where other performance concerns exist, OR
  2. 2The loading function for the incipient overtopping location is significantly different than the loading function where performance concerns exist
Minimum Requirement

At a minimum, users must provide Elevations and AEP estimates for the levee toe (0% load), 100% load, and 100% load plus 3 feet.

The LST will fit a semi-logarithmic curve through those points to complete the hazard curve for the full range of events. However, you can enter stage-frequency pairs to better define the curve if they are readily available for the levee segment in question.

Loading functions (stage-frequency) for levees typically trend toward a semi-logarithmic shape. This comes from an analytical Log-Pearson Type III (LP3) flow frequency curve, which is approximately log-normal for skew values close to zero and exactly log-normal for zero skew. Stage-discharge curves for prismatic channels tend to have a shape consistent with a second order polynomial. The combination of the flow frequency curve and the stage-discharge curve produces a stage-frequency curve that is approximately semi-logarithmic. Many other factors, including upstream regulation and channel geometry, can affect the shape of a flood hazard curves. In general, a semi-logarithmic shape is relatively common.

On the Loading Functions tab, enter the following information:

  1. Must be different than the other loading function names for this segment
  2. The geospatial location can be selected by clicking on the "pin" icon and selecting a location in the map window. The location is for informational and organizational purposes only, it does not impact the compute.
  3. Comments should include justification on the use of one or more loading functions and how the loading function was derived.
  4. A Loading Function for one or more locations along the levee segment

The specific Flood Probability tab inputs are described in more detail below.

AEP of water surface elevation at the levee toe (0%)​

The AEP for water surface elevation at the levee toe is the AEP at which the levee system would first experience a flood loading large enough to prevent flooding in the leveed area. It is typically based on the elevation of the landside toe. However, care should be taken to make sure the AEP selected represents the point at which the levee (not pre-existing high ground) starts reducing risk in the leveed area.

To determine and enter the representative AEP value:

  1. 1Estimate the levee toe water surface elevation using readily available information. Enter that water surface elevation in the Elevation (ft) column of the table
  2. 2Estimate the AEP for current hydrologic and hydraulic conditions using available historical, discharge-probability, water surface elevation-probability, water surface elevation-discharge, and water surface profile-probability relationships
  3. 3Enter information about the AEP best estimate, hydrologic and hydraulic conditions, data sources, station location, gage location, etc., in the Comments field. If the project contains both embankment and floodwall alignments be sure to note both toe loading best estimates in the Comments field.'

AEP for additional water surface elevations (if available)​

The AEP for additional water surface elevations, (e.g. 25%, 50%, and 75% of the total levee height) can provide a more accurate estimate of the stage–frequency relationship of water on the levee. Additional entries can lead to a better estimate of overall risk, especially if the loading function for the site in question is not well represented by a semi-logarithmic curve. To determine and enter additional representative AEP values:

  1. 1Determine the levee's current representative overall height
    If only one loading function is entered, use the distance from toe to top that is greatest along the segment. If multiple loading function are entered, use a representative height for the portion that is represented by the loadingfunction. This information should be based on original design document, as-built drawings, or other available information.
  2. 2Estimate the water surface elevations necessary to load the levee to 25%, 50%, and 75% of the total height. Additional points can be entered if the data is readily available.
  3. 3Estimate the ACE for each elevation under current hydrologic and hydraulic conditions using available historical flood records, discharge-probability, water surface elevation-probability, elevation-discharge, and water surface profile-probability relationships.'

Note the ACE best estimate, hydrologic and hydraulic conditions, data sources, station location, etc., in the Comments field, ensuring all elevations are in NAVD88. If the project contains both embankment and floodwall alignments be sure to note both best estimates in the Comments field.

AEP at top of levee (100%)​

The AEP for the top of levee is the AEP for overtopping at the location for subject loading function. If only one loading function is used for the segment, the AEP at top of levee must be the best estimate AEP for incipient overtopping of the segment, which is the AEP for when the levee segment would first begin to overtop. To determine and enter the representative AEP value:

Estimate the stage for overtopping by comparing water surface profiles under current hydrologic and hydraulic conditions and corresponding elevation surveys of the current top of levee from the levee profile. If this is not possible, follow these guidelines:

  1. 1When detailed profile data are not available, base the estimate on a comparison of available stage and top-of-levee information.
    1. aConsider the potential existence and location of low spots in the top-of-levee profile either by design due to superiority or resulting from settlement, modification, and other changes that may have occurred since construction of the levee system.
    2. bConsider whether efforts to raise low spots in the levee are likely to be successful. If so, use the height, and associated AEP, for the incipient overtopping estimate.
    3. cAlso consider potential changes in water surface elevation and profile characteristics that may have occurred since construction of the levee system due to changes in the watershed caused by upstream and/or downstream regulation, such as flood control, hydropower and navigation dams, sedimentation, vegetation, and the like to determine if the incipient overtopping location may be located somewhere other than the design location.
  2. 2When no data or information on incipient overtopping are readily available, use an estimate of the top of levee elevation at or near the downstream limit of the levee segment if it is reasonable to assume that first overtopping will occur near this location.
  3. 3Use the location of a project gage if the benefit of having higher quality information at the gage site is judged to outweigh the potential benefits of identifying precisely the location of first overtopping.
  4. 4Base the ACE estimate on current hydrologic and hydraulic conditions using available historical flood records, discharge-probability, water surface elevation-probability, elevation-discharge, and water surface profile-probability relationships.

Note the AEP best estimate, hydrologic and hydraulic conditions, data sources, station location, etc., in the Comments field, ensuring all elevations are in NAVD88. If the project contains both embankment and floodwall alignments be sure to note both best estimates in the Comments field.

AEP at top of levee plus 3 ft (100% + 3 ft)​

The AEP for the top of levee plus 3 ft is the AEP for a very large overtopping event at the location for subject loading function. Similar to values below top of levee (100%), the LST will use the top of levee and top of levee plus 3 ft data to fit a semi-logarithmic function to define the likelihood for the full range of events that overtop the levee. The location for this estimate should be the same as what was used for the overtopping estimate (100%).

Estimate the stage for overtopping plus 3 ft by comparing water surface profiles under current hydrologic and hydraulic conditions and corresponding elevation surveys of the current top of levee from the levee profile. In many cases, water surface elevations and corresponding AEPs will not be available for events that overtop the levee by 3 feet. In that case, use judgement to extrapolate the existing information, while taking into consideration specific information about the site (e.g. are there other terrain features that limit the ability of overtopping flows to spread out such as narrow valley walls? etc.). In many cases, an estimate one order of magnitude less frequent than the AEP of overtopping may be reasonable if no other data exists.

Note the AEP best estimate, hydrologic and hydraulic conditions, data sources, station location, etc., in the Comments field, ensuring all elevations are in NAVD88. If the project contains both embankment and floodwall alignments be sure to note both best estimates in the Comments field.
